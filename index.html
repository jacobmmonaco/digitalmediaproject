About This Project

This website was created by Jacob Monaco for HIST 110 at San Diego Miramar College (Spring 2025). It is a digital history project that explores how nuclear weapons transformed military strategy after 1945.

This website is based on the thesis that nuclear weapons shifted global strategy from conventional warfare to a doctrine centered on deterrence, strategic posturing, and diplomacy. Each section focuses on one of these key transformations and includes information from primary and secondary sources.

Key Events

  • 1939–1945 – The Manhattan Project secretly develops the first atomic bombs.
  • March 1945 – Tokyo firebombing kills an estimated 100,000 civilians in one night.
  • August 6 & 9, 1945 – The U.S. drops atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, killing over 200,000 combined.
  • 1946 – U.S. Strategic Bombing Survey warns that American cities are just as vulnerable to atomic attacks.
  • 1950 – NSC-68 proposes nuclear deterrence and arms buildup as national strategy.
  • 1953 – Eisenhower delivers the "Atoms for Peace" speech to the UN, advocating peaceful nuclear technology.
  • 1961 – The Soviet Union detonates Tsar Bomba, the largest nuclear weapon in history (50 megatons).
  • October 1962 – Cuban Missile Crisis brings the world to the brink of nuclear war.
  • October 27, 1962 – Soviet officer Vasili Arkhipov prevents nuclear torpedo launch during crisis, averting global war.
  • 1986 – Global nuclear arsenals peak at over 70,000 warheads.
  • 1987 – The INF Treaty eliminates an entire class of intermediate-range nuclear weapons.
  • 2024 – Over 12,000 nuclear warheads remain active globally, despite decades of arms control efforts.

WWII Firebombings & Introducing the Bomb

The race to develop the atomic bomb began between the Allies and the Nazis. In the earlier stages of World War II, scientists including Albert Einstein feared that Nazi Germany was making plans to develop a nuclear weapon. This led to the U.S. initiating the Manhattan Project to develop one first, bringing the smartest scientists in the world together leading to the first successful detonation of an atomic bomb by the United States in 1945.

The destruction unleashed by these new weapons was like nothing before. But to understand why they changed the nature of war, it helps to compare them to the most destructive conventional tactic of the time: the Tokyo firebombings. During that assault, hundreds of American B-29 bombers dropped explosives that turned Tokyo into a firestorm, killing roughly 100,000 people in a single night.

Just months later, a single atomic bomb dropped on Hiroshima instantly killed 70,000 people and over 140,000 by year’s end. Yet Hiroshima was a much smaller city than Tokyo. With a population of 345,000 compared to Tokyo’s 6.7 million, the bomb’s potential for destruction was immensely greater.

The atomic bomb revolutionized military strategy. Unlike conventional bombings, which required hundreds of planes and hours of destruction, the bomb achieved devastation with one aircraft and one payload. It wasn’t just a weapon it was a new threat to all existing life on the planet.

Brinkmanship and the Edge of War

As the Cold War escalated, nuclear weapons were no longer viewed as tools of warfare, but as symbols of power. Both the United States and Soviet Union built massive nuclear arsenals and used them as leverage. This strategy of brinkmanship, where each side pursued immensely dangerous strategy to the edge of nuclear war, defined global conflict during the middle of the 20th century.

The most dangerous example came in October 1962: the Cuban Missile Crisis. When U.S. intelligence confirmed that the Soviet Union was installing nuclear missiles in Cuba, capable of striking nearly every major city in the U.S. within minutes, President John F. Kennedy addressed the nation. He described the act as “a deliberately provocative and unjustified change in the status quo” and demanded immediate removal of the missiles. Upon refusal, the U.S. initiated a naval blockade of Cuba, and military forces reached a level of nuclear readiness higher than ever before or since. The world held its breath as two superpowers stood on the brink of nuclear war.

In the end, diplomacy prevailed: the Soviets agreed to remove the missiles from Cuba in exchange for a U.S. promise not to invade the island, and a secret agreement to remove American missiles from Turkey. No weapons were fired, yet the threat of mutual destruction forced both sides to back down.

Just two years earlier, the Soviet Union conducted the most powerful nuclear test in history: Tsar Bomba. With a yield over 3,000 times greater than the bomb dropped on Hiroshima, it was more demonstration of power than weapon. A witness described it as "it seemed to suck the whole Earth into it."

Brinkmanship showed that nuclear weapons were no longer actually meant to be used, they were meant to be seen, feared, and negotiated with. War strategy shifted from battles to brinkmanship, with the threat of total annihilation always hovering in the background.

Diplomacy and Arms Control

As the world dealt with the realization of the power of nuclear weapons, nations began to prioritize diplomacy over warfare. One of the earliest of these efforts came in 1953, when President Dwight D. Eisenhower delivered his “Atoms for Peace” speech at the United Nations. He proposed ideas of sharing nuclear technology for peaceful purposes, hoping to shift the global narrative of the power of nuclear energy all together.

Inside the U.S., classified documents like NSC-68 argued that military strength alone wasn’t enough. To prevent nuclear war, America needed to project economic and political strength while maintaining a military edge. This thinking shaped Cold War diplomacy and led to key arms control efforts in the decades that followed.

One of the most significant breakthroughs came in 1987, when President Ronald Reagan and Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev signed the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces (INF) Treaty. For the first time, two superpowers agreed to eliminate an entire category of nuclear missiles. The treaty marked a shift from preparing to fight nuclear wars to preventing them altogether.

But diplomacy hasn’t always come from treaties or world leaders. In 1962, during the peak of the Cuban Missile Crisis, a single man aboard a Soviet submarine may have saved the world. As U.S. ships dropped warning charges to force the sub to surface, Captain Valentin Savitsky gave the order to launch a nuclear torpedo. He needed unanimous approval from two other officers. One said yes, but the other, Vasili Arkhipov, said no. His refusal stopped a potential nuclear war. For a moment, the fate of the world depended on one man’s restraint.

These efforts, both formal and personal, helped bring global nuclear stockpiles down from their peak of over 70,000 warheads in 1986 to about 12,100 today. However, today’s arsenals are far more advanced, and thousands of warheads remain on hair-trigger alert. The world has moved closer to peace—but the shadow of nuclear war has never fully disappeared.

Key Figures

Vasili Arkhipov

A Soviet naval officer who stopped a nuclear torpedo launch during the Cuban Missile Crisis.

In 1962, while deep underwater and cut off from Moscow, Arkhipov refused to authorize a nuclear strike that could have triggered World War III. His calm judgment is credited with saving millions of lives.

John F. Kennedy

U.S. president during the Cuban Missile Crisis who helped prevent nuclear war through careful diplomacy.

Kennedy’s televised speech alerted Americans to Soviet missiles in Cuba. His negotiation with Khrushchev led to mutual de-escalation and became a model for nuclear crisis management.

Dwight D. Eisenhower

U.S. president and former general who proposed peaceful uses for nuclear technology.

In his 1953 "Atoms for Peace" speech to the United Nations, Eisenhower promoted the idea of international nuclear cooperation for energy and medicine—planting seeds for future arms control.

Ronald Reagan

U.S. president who signed the INF Treaty with the Soviet Union, reducing nuclear arsenals.

In 1987, Reagan and Gorbachev agreed to eliminate all intermediate-range nuclear missiles, marking one of the most successful arms control agreements in history.

Albert Einstein

Renowned physicist whose 1939 letter to President Roosevelt helped initiate the U.S. atomic bomb program.

Though not directly involved in the Manhattan Project, Einstein’s warning that Nazi Germany might build a bomb first pushed U.S. leadership to act. He later became a strong advocate for nuclear disarmament.

Mikhail Gorbachev

Soviet leader who signed the INF Treaty with Reagan, reducing Cold War nuclear tensions.

Gorbachev played a pivotal role in de-escalating the arms race. His reforms and openness to negotiation led to landmark agreements that reduced nuclear weapons on both sides.

J. Robert Oppenheimer

Scientific director of the Manhattan Project, known as the “father of the atomic bomb.”

Oppenheimer oversaw the creation of the first atomic weapons. After the war, he became an advocate for arms control, famously saying, “Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds.”

Harry S. Truman

U.S. president who authorized the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945.

Truman made the decision to use nuclear weapons against Japan in an effort to end World War II. His choice marked the beginning of the nuclear age and remains one of history’s most controversial decisions.

Nikita Khrushchev

Soviet Premier during the Cuban Missile Crisis, responsible for placing missiles in Cuba.

Khrushchev’s bold move to install nuclear weapons in Cuba brought the world to the brink of war. He ultimately agreed to withdraw the missiles in exchange for U.S. concessions, helping avoid nuclear conflict.
